Building this site

My first experience with HTML and CSS came from a free course on Codecademy, which helped me learn the basics of the two. I then took a Udacity course on responsive web design. This taught me to first design the site for mobile and scale up from there using media queries to make the site responsive.

This responsive design can be seen mainly on the 'KSP tab' of the site. The content flows as the page is re-sized allowing it to look good on all devices. The design of the site is simple. The content for each tab corresponds to a <section> element. Contained within each section are a number of <article>s that get displayed in lighter grey boxes.

I chose a darker themed site as I think it looks better. I am thinking about adding a switch for a light mode but haven't worked out how to do this yet. Initially the color was a plain dark grey, but I noticed that adding a little bit of blue made it look less bland.

I looked at various ways to do the tabs using just CSS but I struggled to combine this with the design. In the end I opted for a bit of JavaScript to set the display style property of the section elements in order to make the tabs function. I also added a JavaScript calculator on the third tab and experimented with some responsive styling on that.

I used the -webkit-scrollbar webkit specific CSS element to test out the browser specific style elements. In Chrome the scrollbar design is in keeping with the dark (with a bit of blue) theme.

Real Rockets in Kerbal Space Program

This page is about Kerbal Space Program (KSP), a rocket simulator with a realistic physics engine. I took rockets from the real world an recreated then in KSP, trying to make them as realsic as possible, from the engine and stage count, to the propellant used and the overall design of the rocket.

Each section is about a different rocket, and has two pictures. The picture of the left is of the real rocket, and the one on the right is my KSP replica. There is also some info about the rocket on the right or under the picures, depending on the size of your brouser.

Space Shuttle

The Space Shuttle is one of the most recognisable rockets in history, this particular image is of STS-120, flown by Space Shuttle Discovery on 23/10/2007, carrying the Harmony module (node 2) to the ISS.

In order to get Discovery to the ISS, the Space Shuttle used 3 RS-25 engines fueled by LH2/LOX, and two solid rocket boosters attached on either side of the large orange tank. The KSP replica uses a white tank, just like the first two Shuttle missions. The white paint was removed after STS-2, increasing the payload capacity.

Space Shuttle (Orbiter)

On the left is a picture of the Space Shuttle Atlantis shortly before landing at the KSC following STS-122. On the right is the KSP replica, firing it's OME (Orbital Maneuvering Engines) as is prepares for its journey back home.

Delta V Heavy

Since the retirement of the Space Shuttle in 2011, the Delta V Heavy is now the largest capacity rocket in operation. At $400 million per launch, the Delta V heavy can get a payload of 28,790kg to LEO (Low Earth Orbit), using 628,420kg of propellant in the process.

Both the real rocket and the KSP (Kerbal Space Program) replica use LH2/LOX to fuel both stages, but unlike the real rocket, the replica includes fuel pumps leading from the side boosters to the center core. The Falcon Heavy will feature simlar fuel crossfeed, but seeing as this is much harder to implement in real life, only 6/9 engines in the center core will be feuled from the side boosters.

Atlas V 551

The Atlas V is a 2 stage rocket operated by ULA (United Launch Alliance). The first stage uses a single RD-180 engine, fueled by RP-1/LOX, while the second stage uses LH2/LOX to fuel its RL10 engine.

Perhaps what is most interesting about the Atlas V is the configuration of its SRBs (Solid Rocket Boosters). As seen here, there SRBs are asymmetrical, meaning the thrust does not line up with the center of mass. This would cause the rocket to rotate, but the engine on the first stage can gimbal (pivot) to cancel out this rotation.

Saturn 1B

The Saturn 1B played an important part in getting the first men on the moon. It's resemblance to the Saturn V is no coincident; much of the hardware used on the Saturn V was tested on the Saturn 1B, such as the CSM (Command/Service Module), the LM (Lunar Module) and the S-IVB upper stage. It flew the Apollo 5 mission, and sent crew to Skylab, an old America space station.

Just the the real thing, the KSP replica has 8 engines in its first stage burning RP-1/LOX and a single engine burning LH2/LOX in the S-IVB upper stage. The replica can be seen shortly after lift off, taking the CSM to orbit.

Orbital Velocity Calculator

Enter your orbital height (in meters) in the box below, and the orbital velocity will be displayed. Orbital velocity is the speed an object needs to be travelling at to maintain its altitude above the earth with no propulsion.

Velocity: 7672 m/s
Orbital Period: 92 minutes

The ISS orbits at an altitude of 100km, with a velocity of 7672 m/s and takes 92 minutes to orbit the Earth.

The moon orbits at about 400,000km, taking 28 days on to orbit, and has a velocity of just 990 m/s.
